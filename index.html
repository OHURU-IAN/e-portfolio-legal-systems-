Course Purpose

Course Purpose (Academic Summary)

This course explains how law works in society and why it matters for everyday life. It shows how legal systems maintain order, settle disputes, and protect people's rights. We explored how law connects with social values like justice, fairness, and equality, and how it shapes the relationship between citizens, the state, and political institutions. The course also introduces both Kenyan and international legal systems, helping us understand how laws are created, applied, and interpreted in different contexts.

My Perspective (Short Reflection)

At first, I expected this course to just explain basic legal concepts, but it ended up giving me a much deeper understanding of how law, society, and politics connect. I now see why legal systems matter for every citizen, not just lawyers. Learning about rights, responsibilities, and how laws are created and applied helped me understand the bigger picture of how a country functions. This course is important for my programme and for life in Kenya because it teaches awareness, accountability, and how to engage with issues that affect society every day.

Learning Outcomes (LO1-LO4)

LO1 - Content

Definitions of law | law and society | norms & social control
  • Short explanation of how the course defined law: The course explained law as a set of formal rules created by the state to guide behaviour, maintain order, protect rights, and resolve disputes in society.
  • Difference between informal norms and formal legal rules: Informal norms like mores and folkways are unwritten cultural expectations we follow out of habit or tradition, while formal legal rules are written laws that the government can enforce through institutions like the police and courts.
  • One example of law as social control or dispute resolution: An example of law as social control is how traffic laws regulate road behaviour and prevent accidents. They help keep order and ensure safety for everyone.
Reflection for LO1

One idea that changed how I think about law was learning the difference between social norms and formal legal rules. I realised that many things we follow in daily life, like manners or cultural expectations, are not "laws," but they still guide behaviour. What stood out most was understanding law as a form of social control that helps keep order. For example, even something as simple as traffic laws shows how society uses rules to prevent chaos and protect people. This made me start noticing how law quietly operates in the background of everyday life.

LO2 - Content Responses

Substantive vs procedural | public vs private | civil vs criminal
  • Substantive vs procedural law (with examples):
    Substantive law defines people's rights and duties, such as criminal offences or contract rights. Procedural law explains the steps and processes used to enforce those rights, like how a case is filed in court or how evidence is handled.
  • Public vs private law (with examples):
    Public law deals with issues between individuals and the state, such as constitutional law or criminal law. Private law deals with relationships between individuals or organisations, like contract law or family law.
  • Civil vs criminal law (difference in purpose/proof):
    Criminal law punishes offences against the state and requires proof beyond reasonable doubt. Civil law resolves disputes between individuals and usually requires proof on a balance of probabilities.
LO2 - Reflection

I find criminal law the most interesting because it shows how society responds to wrongdoing and protects public safety. Hearing real cases in Kenya, especially those involving corruption or violent crime, made me realise how important fair investigation and evidence are. It also connects to my programme because understanding crime, justice, and accountability helps me think more critically about society and decision-making.

LO3 - Sources of Law (Kenya & Beyond)

Constitution | Acts of Parliament | common law | customary & religious law | international law
  • Main sources of Kenyan law (in order): Constitution (supreme law), Acts of Parliament, delegated legislation, judicial precedent (common law/doctrine of stare decisis), African customary law where applicable, and Islamic law in personal matters for Muslims.
  • Where African customary law and Islamic law apply: Customary law applies in communities where it is not repugnant to justice and morality, often in family, land, or succession matters. Islamic law applies mainly in personal status issues (marriage, divorce, inheritance) for consenting Muslims.
  • International law and treaties: Ratified treaties become part of Kenyan law under Article 2(6) of the Constitution. International norms can influence court decisions and legislation, especially in human rights and cross-border matters.
Reflection for LO3

Understanding where law comes from helps ordinary Kenyans know their rights, duties, and the limits of state power. Legal pluralism (recognising constitutional, statutory, customary, religious, and international sources) can be a strength because it respects diversity, but it also creates challenges when systems conflict. Seeing how these sources interact makes me more aware of how justice is balanced in everyday life.

LO4 - Content

Applying legal knowledge | constitution & rights | international & religious perspectives
  • Rights, duties and public participation under the Kenyan Constitution:
    I learned that the Constitution protects basic rights such as equality, freedom of expression, and access to justice, while also giving citizens duties like obeying the law, respecting others, and participating in national processes like elections and public discussions.
  • Key ideas about international law / human rights:
    International law promotes global standards for human rights, peace, and cooperation between countries. It helps guide how nations treat people and solve issues that cross borders.
  • Other legal traditions (religious or customary law):
    We also studied how systems like religious law and African customary law handle justice. These traditions influence family matters, conflict resolution, and moral expectations in some communities.

Supreme Court Class Trip

What I observed

During our visit to the Judiciary Museum of Kenya-located in the basement of the Supreme Court of Kenya building-I discovered there is much more to the court than simply courtrooms. Ahead of our tour I did not realise the museum existed. Inside, we encountered exhibits showing traditional dispute resolution (a Kikuyu mugumo tree scene), historical artefacts, and displays about freedom fighters like Dedan Kimathi and the Kapenguria Six. The museum captured Kenya's judicial history-from customary systems, through colonial courts, to contemporary constitutional law.

Connecting to My Programme

  • For business: Stability and clear dispute-resolution mechanisms (like those embodied in the Supreme Court's role) support investor confidence and ethical governance.
  • For IT: The museum and court infrastructure hinted at how digital systems (e-filing, case-management, archives) might support transparency and access to justice in Kenya.
  • For journalism: The exhibits reinforced the importance of media access, historical records, and how understanding legal tradition strengthens accurate reporting and informed public discussion.
Personal reaction

I felt both surprised and impressed during the visit. I did not expect a dedicated museum inside the Supreme Court, and seeing how legal history is displayed-from tree-based dispute resolution to colonial courts to modern constitutional justice-shifted my view of law. The visit increased my trust in the justice system's ability to evolve and serve the people. It made me realise that justice is not only about what happens in the courtroom-it is about culture, history, access, and how ordinary citizens engage with law.
